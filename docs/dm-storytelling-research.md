# DM Storytelling Research & Techniques Guide

A practical reference for distilling professional DM storytelling techniques into AI DM prompt instructions. Drawn from Matt Colville ("Running the Game"), Matt Mercer's DM tips, The Angry GM, Sly Flourish's "Return of the Lazy Dungeon Master", Robin Laws' "Robin's Laws of Good Game Mastering", Brennan Lee Mulligan's techniques, and professional improv (UCB, Second City).

---

## 1. Narrative Structure

### The Three-Act Structure Adapted for TTRPG Sessions

Traditional three-act structure maps cleanly onto a single TTRPG session, but requires adaptation because the "audience" is also the protagonist.

**Act 1 — Setup (first 15-20% of session)**
- Establish the situation: where are we, what just happened, what's at stake right now.
- Reground the player in the world. Remind them (through environmental detail, not exposition) of unresolved threads.
- Present the session's central tension or question: a problem to solve, a place to explore, a threat to confront.
- Matt Colville's advice: "Start with something happening." Never open with nothing going on.

**Act 2 — Confrontation (60-70% of session)**
- Rising action through escalating complications. Each obstacle should be harder or more complex than the last.
- The midpoint turn: something shifts. New information arrives, an ally reveals a secret, the enemy adapts. The player's initial plan is no longer sufficient.
- Sly Flourish's principle: "Let the story develop at the table." Don't over-plan Act 2 — let player choices create the complications.
- Multiple decision points. Every 2-3 exchanges should present a meaningful choice.

**Act 3 — Resolution or Cliffhanger (final 15-20%)**
- The session's central tension resolves (or transforms into a new, bigger question).
- If resolving: show consequences. The world changed because of what the player did.
- If cliffhanging: end on a revelation, an impossible choice, or a new threat emerging. Brennan Lee Mulligan's technique: end mid-sentence if it serves the drama.
- Never end in a dead zone — always leave the player wanting to come back.

### Rising Action in Practice

Each scene within a session should follow its own micro-arc:
- **ESTABLISH**: Set the stage (2-3 sensory details).
- **DEVELOP**: Present the situation, let the player interact, introduce complications.
- **RESOLVE or CLIFFHANG**: Close with a consequence, a transition, or a hook into the next scene.

Stack these micro-arcs so each one raises the stakes slightly. Scene 1 establishes normalcy. Scene 2 introduces trouble. Scene 3 escalates. Scene 4 forces a hard choice. Scene 5 delivers consequences.

## 2. Scene-Setting

### Sensory-Rich Descriptions

The Angry GM's "Three Pillars of Description": every scene needs at least two senses, always including sight plus one other. Three senses makes a scene vivid. Four is lush. Five is reserved for transcendent moments.

**Sight** — The foundation. Lighting conditions, colors, movement, scale. "The chamber is vast — cathedral-high ceilings lost in shadow. Bioluminescent moss traces the walls in pale green veins."

**Sound** — The most underused sense. Ambient noise grounds reality. Silence is itself a sound cue (and an ominous one). "The only sound is dripping water, irregular, like a heartbeat that keeps losing rhythm."

**Smell** — Instantly evocative and tied to memory. Use sparingly but with specificity. Not "it smells bad" but "the air tastes of copper and wet stone." Decay, smoke, rain on dust, iron, pine, sweat, old paper.

**Touch/Temperature** — Creates physical immersion. Cold stone under bare feet. The heat of a forge that makes armor uncomfortable. Rough bark, smooth marble, the slick surface of something that shouldn't be wet.

**Taste** — Rare but powerful. Salt air near the coast. Metallic tang of blood after a blow. The sweetness of ceremonial wine. The grit of sand in a desert wind.

### Environmental Storytelling

Locations should tell stories without exposition:
- Claw marks on a door tell you something tried to get in (or out).
- A half-eaten meal says someone left in a hurry.
- Fresh flowers on an old grave say someone still cares.
- A child's toy in a burned village says more than any NPC monologue.

Robin Laws' principle: "The environment is a character." It has mood, history, and intent. A forest that's been logged on one side and left wild on the other tells a political story.

### Making Locations Distinct

Every location needs a "sensory anchor" — one unique detail that no other location shares:
- The market where every vendor whispers instead of shouting.
- The temple where the floor is warm regardless of season.
- The tavern where the ceiling is too low and everyone hunches.
- The canyon where sound echoes three times, each echo slightly different.

## 3. NPC Voice

### Distinct Speech Patterns

Matt Mercer's core technique: every NPC needs at least ONE distinguishing vocal characteristic. This doesn't mean doing accents — it means varying:

- **Vocabulary level**: A scholar uses polysyllabic words. A farmer uses simple, concrete language. A noble uses formal constructions. A street kid uses slang.
- **Sentence length**: Terse characters speak in fragments. "Don't. Not worth it." Verbose characters unspool long, winding sentences that circle back on themselves.
- **Speech rhythm**: Some characters pause mid-thought. Others rush. Some repeat themselves. Some never finish a sentence before starting the next one.
- **Verbal tics**: "You understand?" tacked onto every statement. Beginning every sentence with "Listen..." Trailing off with "...but what do I know?"
- **What they WON'T say**: A character who never uses names (always "you" or "that one"). A character who never says "I" (always "we" or "one"). A character who never directly answers a question.

### Motivations That Drive Behavior

Brennan Lee Mulligan's framework: every NPC wants something RIGHT NOW (immediate goal) and something ALWAYS (core drive). The tension between these creates depth.

- Immediate goal: "I want to sell you this sword."
- Core drive: "I want to leave this town and never come back."
- Resulting behavior: The NPC oversells, seems desperate, drops the price too fast. The player senses something is off.

### Making NPCs Memorable in Few Lines

The "Three Things" rule (from improv): establish three things about an NPC in their first appearance:
1. One physical detail (limps, massive hands, never blinks)
2. One behavioral detail (laughs before speaking, counts coins obsessively, stands too close)
3. One emotional detail (bitter about something specific, unexpectedly kind, clearly afraid)

These three things are repeated in every subsequent appearance, creating consistency and recognition.

## 4. Pacing

### Long Description vs. Quick Dialogue

Sly Flourish's pacing rule: **description length should match narrative importance.**

- **Long descriptions** (3-5 sentences): First arrival at a new location. A major reveal. The moment before a boss fight. The aftermath of tragedy. These are "camera slow" moments.
- **Medium descriptions** (1-2 sentences): Standard scene transitions. Entering a room. Meeting a known NPC. Combat round descriptions.
- **Quick dialogue** (single lines): NPC banter. Rapid exchanges. Combat trash-talk. Information delivered in passing.

### Tension and Relief Cycles

The Angry GM's "Dramatic Pacing Curve": never sustain high tension for more than 3-4 consecutive exchanges. The player needs breathing room or they become numb to danger.

Pattern: **Tension → Tension → PEAK → Relief → Build → Tension → PEAK → Relief**

- After combat: offer a quiet moment. A campfire. A conversation. A discovery that's beautiful rather than threatening.
- After emotional intensity: let the player process. Don't immediately introduce the next crisis.
- After a major revelation: pause. Let the player ask questions. Let them sit with it.

### The Five-Room Dungeon

A pacing framework (from Johnn Four) that structures any adventure into five beats:

1. **Entrance/Guardian**: An obstacle that establishes the dungeon's theme. Not necessarily combat — could be a puzzle, a social challenge, or an environmental hazard.
2. **Puzzle/Roleplay**: A challenge that can't be solved by fighting. Rewards creativity and lateral thinking.
3. **Trick/Setback**: Something goes wrong. A trap, a betrayal, a complication that changes the player's plan.
4. **Boss/Climax**: The main challenge. The hardest fight, the most critical negotiation, the biggest decision.
5. **Reward/Revelation**: The payoff. Loot, information, a story advancement. But also a hook — the reward reveals something new that demands attention.

### When to Cut vs. Linger

Cut when:
- The player has made their decision and the outcome is clear.
- A conversation has reached its natural conclusion.
- You're about to repeat information the player already has.
- Staying would reduce tension rather than build it.

Linger when:
- The player is emotionally engaged and processing.
- The environment has story to tell.
- A quiet moment would make the next tense moment land harder.
- The player is asking questions — they want to be here.

## 5. Player Agency

### Meaningful Choices

Robin Laws' hierarchy of choices:
1. **Strategic choices**: Affect the direction of the campaign. Which faction to ally with. Whether to confront or avoid.
2. **Tactical choices**: Affect the approach to a specific challenge. Sneak or fight. Negotiate or threaten.
3. **Expressive choices**: Don't change outcomes but let the player define their character. How they treat a prisoner. What they say to a dying enemy.

All three matter. Strategic choices drive plot. Tactical choices drive engagement. Expressive choices drive investment.

### Presenting Choices

Never present choices as a menu ("Do you want to A, B, or C?"). Instead:
- Describe the situation with enough detail that the player sees the options themselves.
- Include environmental cues that suggest possibilities: "The window is unlatched. The guard is looking the other way. Your blade is drawn."
- Let the player invent options you didn't plan for. The best choices are the ones the player thinks of, not the ones the DM prepared.

### "Yes, And..." Improv Technique

From UCB and Second City improv:
- **Accept the premise**: Whatever the player declares, it's real. They said it; it happened (or they attempted it).
- **Add to it**: Build on their declaration. Don't just confirm — expand. "Yes, and as you grab the chandelier, you notice the chain is rusted and groaning."
- **Never "No, but..."** as a default. Reserve "No" for the physically impossible, and even then, offer alternatives: "You can't fly, but you notice the scaffolding goes nearly to the ceiling."

### Making Failures Interesting

Matt Colville's principle: "Failure should change the situation, not end it."

- Bad failure: "You fail to pick the lock. It's still locked." (Nothing happened. Boring.)
- Good failure: "The pick snaps inside the lock with a metallic PING that echoes down the corridor. You hear boots." (Failure created a new situation.)

Every failure should either:
1. Create a new problem (alert guards, break equipment, reveal position)
2. Reveal new information (the lock is magical, the wall is hollow, the NPC is lying)
3. Change the stakes (time pressure increases, resources diminish, allies are endangered)

## 6. Combat Narration

### Cinematic vs. Mechanical Combat

The difference between flat and vivid combat is specificity:

- **Mechanical**: "You hit the goblin for 8 damage."
- **Cinematic**: "Your blade catches the goblin across the ribs — it shrieks and stumbles back, clutching its side. Dark blood between its fingers."

Rules for cinematic combat narration:
1. Name the body part. Not "you hit" but "your blade finds the gap in its shoulder guard."
2. Show the enemy's reaction. Pain, surprise, rage, fear. Enemies are living things, not HP pools.
3. Use the environment. Describe where combatants are in relation to terrain. "It slams into the table, scattering dice and tankards."
4. Vary the description. Don't use the same phrasing twice in one fight.

### Using Environment in Combat

Every combat location should have at least 2 interactable elements:
- Terrain: high ground, narrow passages, slippery floors, breakable barriers
- Objects: chandeliers to swing from, tables to flip, doors to barricade, barrels to roll
- Hazards: fire, water, unstable ground, falling debris, magical effects
- Escape routes: windows, tunnels, rooftops — combat should never feel like a sealed room unless that's intentional

### NPC Combat Personality

Different enemies fight differently:

- **Cowardly goblins**: Attack in packs, flee when outnumbered, use traps and ambushes, scream for help. When losing: surrender, beg, offer information.
- **Disciplined soldiers**: Hold formation, use coordinated tactics, protect their wounded, follow chain of command. When losing: orderly retreat, regroup, return with reinforcements.
- **Feral beasts**: No tactics, pure aggression, target the weakest or most wounded, disengage if badly hurt. Driven by hunger or territory.
- **Intelligent villains**: Exploit weaknesses, use terrain, target the player's allies, retreat strategically, set traps during combat. They've studied the player.
- **Undead/constructs**: No fear, no morale break, relentless advance, simple tactics executed perfectly. They don't stop until destroyed.

### Describing the Aftermath

Combat doesn't end when HP hits zero. Describe:
- The silence after. The sudden absence of clashing steel.
- The physical cost. Blood, exhaustion, damaged equipment, injuries.
- The emotional cost. Did the player enjoy the killing? Regret it? Fear what comes next?
- The environmental impact. Broken furniture, scorched walls, bodies that need to be dealt with.

## 7. Mystery & Foreshadowing

### Planting Clues Naturally

The Angry GM's "Three-Clue Rule": for any conclusion the player MUST reach, plant at least three independent clues. If the player needs to figure out the butler did it, there should be three separate paths to that conclusion:

1. Physical evidence (muddy boots matching the butler's path)
2. Testimony (a maid saw the butler near the scene)
3. Behavioral clue (the butler's unusual nervousness when the topic arises)

Why three? Because players will miss the first clue, misinterpret the second, and the third gives them enough to connect. One clue = unsolvable mystery. Two clues = frustrating. Three clues = satisfying deduction.

### Building Suspense

Techniques for sustained tension:

- **The Countdown**: Something is happening on a timer. The player knows they have limited time. Every exchange reminds them the clock is ticking — not explicitly, but through environmental cues. "The cracks in the ceiling widen. Dust falls."
- **The Unknown**: Something is there, but the player doesn't know what. Describe effects without causes. Sounds without sources. Shadows that move wrong.
- **The Inevitable**: The player knows what's coming and can't stop it. A betrayal they've figured out but can't prove. An army they can see approaching. A ritual they can't interrupt in time.
- **The Choice**: Two things the player cares about are threatened simultaneously. They can only save one.

### Dramatic Reveals

Brennan Lee Mulligan's reveal technique: the best reveals recontextualize everything the player already knew. Not "here's new information" but "here's what the information you already had ACTUALLY meant."

Structure for reveals:
1. Pause the action. Create a moment of stillness.
2. Deliver the core information in one or two sentences. Don't dilute it.
3. Let the silence hang. Don't immediately explain implications.
4. Let the player react before continuing.

### Foreshadowing Techniques

- **Recurring imagery**: A symbol, color, or motif that appears before every major event. The player starts to dread seeing it.
- **NPC behavior shifts**: A trusted NPC starts acting subtly different. Not enough to alarm — just enough to notice on reflection.
- **Environmental echoes**: The player encounters a ruined version of something they'll later see intact (or vice versa).
- **Prophecy and rumor**: NPCs mention things in passing that become relevant sessions later. "They say the old tower hums on moonless nights." Sessions later: the player is at the old tower on a moonless night.

## 8. Emotional Beats

### Creating Specific Emotions

**Wonder**: Describe something genuinely beautiful, ancient, or vast. Use scale. "The cavern opens into a space so vast that clouds form near the ceiling. Bioluminescent organisms drift through the air like slow stars." Wonder requires slowing down — give it extra description, more sensory detail, longer pauses.

**Dread**: Remove the player's advantages one at a time. Light sources fail. Allies go quiet. Familiar paths lead to unfamiliar places. Dread is the slow removal of safety, not the sudden appearance of danger.

**Humor**: Let it arise from characters and situations, not from the DM cracking jokes. NPC quirks, absurd juxtapositions, the player's own creativity. A fearsome dragon that's afraid of mice. A guard who takes their job far too seriously. Humor works best when it's earned by the world being consistent — the funny thing is funny BECAUSE everything else is serious.

**Triumph**: The key to triumph is suffering that preceded it. A victory is only triumphant if the player genuinely feared they would lose. Set up the triumph by making the challenge seem overwhelming, then let the player find a way through skill, creativity, or sacrifice. Never hand them a triumph — let them earn it.

**Grief**: Requires investment. The player can only grieve what they loved, and they can only love what they've spent time with. Build the relationship across sessions before threatening it. When loss comes, describe it with physical specificity — not "they died" but "they stopped breathing. Their hand went slack in yours."

**Connection**: Quiet moments of genuine warmth between characters. A shared meal. A joke only two people understand. A companion remembering something the player said three sessions ago. Connection is built in the margins, not in grand gestures.

### Knowing When to Be Serious and When to Be Light

Sly Flourish's tonal guideline: "Follow intensity with relief, and relief with intensity."

- After a death: allow a moment of humor (gallows humor is real and human).
- After comedy: introduce a note of seriousness (the funny NPC has a genuine problem).
- After sustained tension: give the player something beautiful or peaceful.
- After peace: remind them that danger exists (a distant sound, a troubling rumor).

### Earning Emotional Payoffs

Emotional payoffs require setup:
- The payoff of a betrayal requires sessions of trust-building.
- The payoff of a sacrifice requires the player to value what was lost.
- The payoff of victory requires genuine risk of failure.
- The payoff of reunion requires painful separation.

The ratio: for every 1 session of emotional payoff, invest 3-5 sessions of setup. Unearned emotion feels manipulative. Earned emotion is unforgettable.

## 9. Session Flow

### Strong Openings

Matt Colville's opening principles:
- **Mid-action**: Drop the player into a situation already in progress. "The door bursts open. Three figures in black rush in. Roll initiative." The player is engaged before they've had time to check their phone.
- **Discovery**: The player wakes up/arrives to find something has changed. "The camp is empty. Your companion's gear is here, but they're gone. There are tracks heading north."
- **Hook**: An NPC arrives with urgent information. A message delivers an impossible claim. Something the player thought was resolved turns out to be unresolved.
- **Callback**: Reference something from a previous session. "Remember the merchant who overcharged you in session 3? He's standing in the town square, surrounded by guards. He sees you and goes pale."

Never start with: "You're in a tavern. What do you do?" without context, tension, or a hook.

### Mid-Session Pacing

The Lazy Dungeon Master approach (Sly Flourish):
- Have 3-5 "scenes" loosely planned, but let the player's choices determine order and emphasis.
- Keep a list of "secrets and clues" — information the player can discover in any scene, not locked to specific locations.
- If the player seems stuck or disengaged, introduce a new element: an NPC arrives, weather changes, a sound from another room, a ticking clock.
- Track energy. If the player's responses are getting shorter, shift to action. If they're writing essays, they're engaged in roleplay — let it breathe.

### Satisfying Endings

Types of session endings (ranked by impact):

1. **The Cliffhanger**: End on a revelation or impossible choice. "The mask falls away. You recognize the face underneath." Cut to black.
2. **The Emotional Beat**: End on a moment of connection, loss, or achievement. A quiet conversation by firelight. A companion's confession. An arrival at a long-sought destination.
3. **The Transition**: End with the player arriving somewhere new. The next session begins in a new location with new possibilities. "The forest thins. Below you, spread across the valley, a city of glass catches the sunset."
4. **The Question**: End with a question that the player will think about between sessions. "The prisoner looks at you and says, 'You really don't know what you are, do you?'"

Never end:
- Mid-combat (unless the combat is the cliffhanger and you can freeze on a dramatic moment).
- Mid-conversation (it feels arbitrary).
- On a lull (the player should leave wanting more, not feeling like it petered out).

## 10. Collaborative Storytelling

### Incorporating Player Backstories

Robin Laws' principle: the player wrote a backstory because they want to see it matter. Use it.

- Mine the backstory for NPCs: family members, rivals, mentors. Bring them into the world.
- Mine it for themes: if the player wrote about loss, create scenarios that echo that theme.
- Mine it for hooks: if the player mentioned a hometown, eventually take the story there.
- Never contradict the backstory without the player's implicit or explicit consent.
- Reveal things about the backstory the player didn't know: "Your mentor? We were friends once. Before what happened at the Sunken Tower."

### Respecting Character Arcs

Players have an arc they're imagining for their character, even if they haven't articulated it. Common arcs:
- **Redemption**: The character is atoning for something. Create opportunities for them to prove they've changed.
- **Coming of age**: The character is learning who they are. Present challenges that test different aspects of identity.
- **Revenge**: The character wants someone to pay. Build toward that confrontation — but make it complicated when they get there.
- **Discovery**: The character is searching for something (truth, family, purpose). Plant breadcrumbs.

The DM's job is not to dictate the arc but to create the CONDITIONS for the arc to unfold. Provide the tests. Let the player decide how their character responds.

### Building on Player Ideas

UCB improv principle: "The best ideas come from the group, not the individual."

When a player says something unexpected:
- Don't panic. Don't say no. Ask yourself: "How can this be true in this world?"
- If the player theorizes about the plot ("I bet the mayor is working with the cult"), consider making it true — or partially true. The player just gave you a gift.
- If the player invents a detail ("My character would know about this because she grew up near a forge"), accept it and build on it. "Yes, and the forge master here recognizes your accent."

Matt Colville's collaboration rule: "You are not the author of the story. You are the author of the WORLD. The player is the author of the STORY."

## Distillation Notes: Converting to AI DM Instructions

When converting these principles into prompt instructions for an AI DM, prioritize:

1. **Concrete rules over abstract principles**: "Include at least 2 senses in every description" is better than "make descriptions vivid."
2. **Templates over guidelines**: Give the AI a structure to follow (ESTABLISH > DEVELOP > RESOLVE) rather than a philosophy to embody.
3. **Measurable constraints**: "Alternate tension and relief every 3-4 exchanges" is enforceable. "Keep good pacing" is not.
4. **Example pairs**: Show BAD vs. GOOD examples for every technique. AI models learn best from contrast.
5. **Checklists**: Before each response, check X, Y, Z. AI models excel at checklist compliance.
6. **Prohibited patterns**: Explicitly list what NOT to do. "Never have two NPCs in the same scene sound identical." "Never start with 'What do you do?' without context."
7. **Emotional calibration**: Give the AI permission to slow down, to linger, to be quiet. Default AI behavior is to fill silence. Good DMing sometimes requires silence.
